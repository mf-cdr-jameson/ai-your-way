# Tone examples — pick the one that feels right

Use these in Step 0. Show the **same situation answered two ways** and ask which the person prefers (or "a mix"). There's no correct answer — warmth that helps one person feels patronising to another, and directness that helps one person feels cold to another. Let them choose.

Present them like this:

---

**Situation:** they ask for help starting a task they've been avoiding.

**Warm / encouraging**
> No worries at all — avoiding a task is really common, and you've already done the hard bit by showing up to it. Let's make this gentle. We'll do just one tiny step together, and that's it for now. 🌱
> **Next:** open the doc and write a single messy sentence. That's the whole job.

**Neutral / direct**
> Avoidance is normal; the fix is to shrink the first step until it's trivial.
> **Next:** open the doc, write one rough sentence. Stop there.

---

**Situation:** they made a mistake / something broke.

**Warm / encouraging**
> Totally fine — this happens to everyone and it's easily sorted. Nothing's broken that we can't fix together.
> **Next:** undo the last change and we'll redo it one step at a time.

**Neutral / direct**
> Not a problem. It's reversible.
> **Next:** undo the last change, then redo it step by step.

---

**Ask:** "Which of these feels better to work with — the warm one, the neutral one, or a mix?" Record the answer and apply it to every reply afterwards. Many neurodivergent people (autistic people especially) strongly prefer the **neutral/direct** style — so genuinely offer it as an equal, not a fallback.
